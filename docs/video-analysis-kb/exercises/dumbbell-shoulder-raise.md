# Dumbbell shoulder raise (front / lateral)

**Equipment:** one or two dumbbells, standing. The front-/lateral-raise family.
**Status:** active — main *alternative* to curl on case 001 (~0.2, pre-reveal).
**Note:** NOT a shoulder press (press flexes the elbow and goes overhead — different exercise).

## Biomechanics

- **Pivot: shoulder.** Arm stays **nearly straight with a fixed soft elbow bend** that does not
  change during the rep — the whole arm moves as one rigid lever.
- **Front raise:** arm lifts **forward** to ~shoulder height, weight at **arm's length in front**.
- **Lateral raise:** arm lifts **out to the side** to shoulder height, weight at **arm's length to
  the side**.
- Grip: pronated/neutral, fixed (no supination sweep).
- Prime movers: anterior deltoid (front) / lateral deltoid + supraspinatus (lateral).
- Lighter loads (long lever), longer arc.

## Egocentric (headband) signature — ranked by what the camera can actually see

1. **"Distant" — apparent size / proximity (best cue):** because the straight arm holds the weight at
   arm's length (~60 cm+ from the lens), it appears **small and distant**, travelling **out over the
   room/floor** and ending **silhouetted against the background** — at the **frame center/horizon**
   (front raise) or the **periphery / out of frame** (lateral). **Minimal background occlusion** and
   **small apparent-size change (weak parallax)**. **Not** large/looming up near the face.
2. Arm stays **straight** (no elbow bend) — best cue in theory, often occluded egocentrically.
3. Hand orientation **pronated/fixed** (back of hand toward camera), no palm rotation.

Contrast: a raise keeps the weight **small and out over the room**; a curl makes it **loom huge and
close** (`dumbbell-biceps-curl.md`). Caveat: depth inferred from monocular size — fragile with tiny
dumbbells / odd stance; the product's IMU rep-clock reads the apex frame where size contrast is
maximal.

## Distinguish from

- **Biceps curl** (`dumbbell-biceps-curl.md`): curl ends **near the body** with a **bent elbow** —
  proximity + elbow are the deciders.
- Front vs lateral raise: front ends in front of the torso; lateral ends at the side/periphery.
