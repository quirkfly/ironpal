# IronPal Kickstarter Campaign — Video Prompts (Body-Mounted Camera)

## Reference Style

Inspired by the Apple product launch video format (as parodied by Joma Tech's "if Apple made..." series): cinematic slow-motion shots, dramatic lighting against dark backgrounds, sweeping close-up reveals, minimalist composition, aspirational narration, smooth transitions, and an overall premium "product as art" energy. The tone should be confident and slightly playful — we're a Kickstarter startup, not actually Apple, so a touch of self-aware enthusiasm keeps it authentic rather than pretentious.

**Brand constants across all videos:**
- Product color: matte black with electric teal accents
- "IronPal" logo in teal (printed on headband side, embroidered on cap side panel, laser-engraved on camera module)
- Teal LED glow from the camera module
- UI/app accents in teal
- Modern gym environments, warm lighting, diverse athletic talent

## All 6 Video Prompts

### Video Prompt 1 — "The Reveal" (Product Launch Hero Video)

**Duration:** 60-90 seconds

**Concept:** A pure Apple-style product reveal. The IronPal camera module emerges from total darkness, lit by a single dramatic spotlight that sweeps across its surface. Then the module is shown clicking into the headband and cap, followed by rapid-fire gym action shots.

**Shot list:**

1. **[0:00-0:05] Black screen.** A single teal dot appears center-frame — the camera module's LED, glowing in darkness. A low, pulsing electronic hum builds.

2. **[0:05-0:15] Macro close-up sweep.** A slow, sweeping camera movement reveals the IronPal camera module on a dark slate surface. Dramatic side lighting catches the anodized aluminum edges, the flush glass lens, the laser-engraved "IronPal" wordmark in teal. Shallow depth of field. Each detail emerges gorgeously — the precision machining, the USB-C port, the micro LED. The feeling: this is a piece of jewelry, not a gadget.

3. **[0:15-0:22] Click-in sequence.** Two rapid product shots: (a) Fingers snap the module into the headband's forehead pocket — a satisfying magnetic click. Close-up of the lens sitting flush in the black fabric, the teal "IronPal" text visible on the side. (b) Same module clicks into the cap's front panel mount. The cap is held by a hand against a dark background, then placed on a model's head. The teal LED pulses once, confirming activation.

4. **[0:22-0:35] Gym montage — cinematic slow motion.** A rapid sequence of 2-3 second shots, all in smooth slow motion with dramatic shallow depth of field:
   - An athlete in the IronPal headband chalking hands at a squat rack, teal LED glowing
   - An athlete in the IronPal cap approaching a lat pulldown machine, grabbing the bar
   - Close-up of hands gripping a barbell, headband visible in the blurred background
   - An athlete mid-deadlift, side angle, cap logo visible
   - A dumbbell being lifted off the rack — the "IronPal" headband on the athlete reflected in the gym mirror

5. **[0:35-0:45] The magic moment — weight detection.** Slow-motion shot of an athlete's hand moving the pin in a weight stack. Cut to: a phone screen showing the IronPal app with the weight ("55 kg") appearing automatically with a smooth teal animation. The gym is blurred behind the phone. The message: the camera saw what you did, automatically.

6. **[0:45-0:55] Workout complete — results.** The athlete sits on a bench, pulls out their phone. The IronPal app shows a fully populated workout log — exercises, weights, reps — all in teal-accented UI cards. The athlete smiles, nods slightly. No typing, no manual input.

7. **[0:55-0:65] Product beauty shots.** Both the headband and cap on a dark surface, lit dramatically, "IronPal" logos visible. The camera module sits between them, glowing LED. The composition is symmetrical and premium.

8. **[0:65-0:75] End card.** Fade to black. The IronPal logo fades in (teal on black). Below it: "Back us on Kickstarter." Teal LED pulse as final beat.

**Music:** Minimalist electronic beat, building slowly — similar to Apple's product reveal scores. Clean, confident, no lyrics. Drops to silence during the weight detection moment for emphasis, then returns for the finale.

**Voiceover (optional, Apple-style):** "We didn't build another fitness tracker. We built the first wearable that sees your workout — every exercise, every weight, every rep. Automatically. This is IronPal."

**Mood:** Premium, mysterious, confident. The product is the star.

### Video Prompt 2 — "Your Workout, Logged Before You Leave" (Problem/Solution Narrative)

**Duration:** 75-90 seconds

**Concept:** A before/after story. The first half shows the frustration of manual workout logging — pausing between sets to type into a phone, forgetting reps, losing track. The second half shows the same workout with IronPal — seamless, automatic, effortless. The contrast makes the value proposition instantly clear.

**Shot list:**

1. **[0:00-0:05] Close-up of a phone screen** showing a cluttered workout spreadsheet app. A thumb hesitates over the screen, then types awkwardly. Cut to the athlete's face — slightly annoyed, mid-set, momentum broken.

2. **[0:05-0:15] "The old way" montage.** Quick cuts:
   - An athlete pauses at the bench press, picks up phone from the floor, squints at the screen
   - An athlete at a cable machine trying to remember: "Was that 12 or 13 reps?"
   - A phone screen showing a half-filled workout log with question marks and typos
   - An athlete walking to the dumbbell rack, then back to their phone, then back to the rack — distracted

   The lighting in these shots is slightly cooler, the color grading slightly desaturated. The mood: this sucks.

3. **[0:15-0:20] Transition.** The athlete reaches for their phone one more time, but instead — they pick up the IronPal headband from their gym bag. Close-up of the "IronPal" logo and the tiny camera module. They put it on. The teal LED lights up. The color grading shifts warmer.

4. **[0:20-0:40] "The IronPal way" montage.** The same exercises, but now the athlete is fully focused, no phone in sight:
   - Bench press — powering through the set, IronPal headband visible, teal LED glowing
   - Cable machine — smooth, uninterrupted set
   - Dumbbell curls — just lifting, not logging
   - Weight stack close-up from the athlete's perspective (simulated POV shot) — the camera sees the pin position and the "50" label

   The music lifts. The lighting is warm, vibrant. The athlete looks in the zone.

5. **[0:40-0:55] The payoff.** Workout's over. The athlete sits on a bench, takes off the headband casually, pulls out their phone. The IronPal app loads automatically:
   - Animated sequence: exercise cards populate one by one — "Bench Press / 80 kg / 3x10 ✓" — "Cable Fly / 25 kg / 3x12 ✓" — "Dumbbell Curl / 16 kg / 3x15 ✓"
   - The athlete's eyebrows raise slightly — impressed. They tap "Save workout."
   - Close-up of the workout summary: total volume, exercise count, session duration. All detected automatically.

6. **[0:55-0:70] Social proof beat.** Quick sequence of different athletes in different gyms — diverse body types, genders, gym settings — all wearing IronPal (some headband, some cap). Each shown mid-exercise, focused, unbothered. The teal LEDs glow across the montage like a visual signature.

7. **[0:70-0:80] End card.** IronPal headband and cap on dark background. Logo. "Your workout, logged before you leave. Back us on Kickstarter."

**Music:** Starts with a slightly awkward, off-beat tone during the "old way" section. Shifts to a confident, driving electronic beat when IronPal enters. Builds to a crescendo during the app reveal.

**Voiceover:** "You go to the gym to train. Not to type. IronPal sees every exercise, reads every weight, counts every rep — so you never have to. Your workout is logged before you leave the gym."

**Mood:** Relatable frustration → relief → excitement. The viewer should think: "That's exactly my problem."

### Video Prompt 3 — "What the Camera Sees" (First-Person POV Showcase)

**Duration:** 60-75 seconds

**Concept:** An educational/demo video showing side-by-side the athlete working out (third-person) and the camera's first-person POV (what the headband/cap actually captures). This directly addresses the question: "Can a body-mounted camera really see enough to track a workout?" The answer is shown, not told.

**Shot list:**

1. **[0:00-0:08] Split screen introduction.** Left half: a male athlete approaches a lat pulldown machine, wearing the IronPal headband. Right half (with a thin teal border): the headband camera's live first-person POV — the lat pulldown machine getting closer, the weight stack visible straight ahead, the numbered plates crisp and readable.

2. **[0:08-0:18] Lat pulldown — weight detection.** Left: the athlete sits down, reaches for the pin, adjusts the weight. Right (POV): close-up of the weight stack from the user's eye level — the hand moving the pin, the number "55" clearly visible next to the pin slot. A subtle teal bracket appears around the weight number in the POV (AI detection hint).

3. **[0:18-0:30] Lat pulldown — exercise and reps.** Left: the athlete performs a set, pulling the bar down smoothly. Right (POV): the bar and cable moving downward in the frame, the weight stack plates lifting in the background, the cyclical motion visible across frames. A teal counter in the corner of the POV ticks up: 1, 2, 3... 10, 11, 12. The exercise name "Lat Pulldown" and weight "55 kg" appear in a teal overlay.

4. **[0:30-0:40] Quick exercise transitions (still split-screen).** Rapid 3-second pairs showing the third-person view and the POV for:
   - Barbell squat: athlete squatting (left) / camera sees the rack, bar, plates from under the bar (right)
   - Dumbbell curl: athlete curling (left) / camera sees the dumbbell in hand, label "16 KG" readable (right)
   - Hack squat machine: athlete on the machine (left) / camera sees the foot platform and plate pegs (right)

5. **[0:40-0:50] The app — everything detected.** Full screen: the IronPal phone app displaying the complete workout log from this session. Each exercise card has a teal checkmark. Weights and reps match what was visible in the POV shots. The camera zooms slowly into the phone screen.

6. **[0:50-0:60] Product shot and end card.** The headband and cap on a dark surface. The camera module between them. "IronPal" logo. "It sees what you see. Back us on Kickstarter."

**Music:** Clean, techy, rhythmic. A driving but not overwhelming beat that matches the split-screen information density. Think: tech product demo music, not gym hype music.

**Voiceover:** "IronPal sees your workout from your perspective. The weight stack — read. The exercise — identified. The reps — counted. All from a tiny camera in your headband. No setup. No gym installation. Just put it on and lift."

**Mood:** Informative, impressive, technical but accessible. The viewer should think: "Oh wow, it actually works — I can see the proof."

### Video Prompt 4 — "Any Gym, Anywhere" (Freedom & Portability)

**Duration:** 60-75 seconds

**Concept:** Emphasizes the unique advantage of the body-mounted approach: it works at any gym without installation. The video shows the same athlete walking into 3-4 different gym environments — a commercial chain gym, a small local gym, a hotel fitness center, an outdoor pull-up bar — and IronPal works seamlessly in every one.

**Shot list:**

1. **[0:00-0:08] Morning.** An athlete in street clothes puts the IronPal cap on, grabs their gym bag, walks out the door. Close-up of the teal "IronPal" logo on the cap's side panel.

2. **[0:08-0:18] Gym 1 — Commercial chain.** The athlete walks into a bright, large commercial gym. Quick shots: scanning a membership card, walking to the machine area, starting a set on the chest press. The IronPal cap is just part of their outfit. The teal LED glows softly.

3. **[0:18-0:28] Gym 2 — Local neighborhood gym.** Different day, different look. The athlete walks into a smaller, grittier gym — exposed brick, older equipment, dim lighting. They go straight to the squat rack. Same IronPal cap. It works here too. Close-up of the weight stack — older, slightly worn labels — but the first-person camera still captures them.

4. **[0:28-0:35] Gym 3 — Hotel fitness center.** The athlete is traveling. A small hotel gym — treadmill, a cable machine, a bench, a dumbbell rack up to 30 kg. They put on the IronPal headband (packed light), do a quick dumbbell session. The headband captures the dumbbell labels perfectly from close range.

5. **[0:35-0:42] Gym 4 — Outdoor.** A park pull-up bar setup. The athlete does pull-ups and bodyweight dips wearing the IronPal cap. The scene is sun-drenched and energetic. Even outdoors, the system tracks exercise and reps via the camera's motion pattern and IMU.

6. **[0:42-0:52] App — unified history.** The athlete sits at a coffee shop after the outdoor session, scrolling through the IronPal app. The workout history shows sessions logged at four different locations — all with complete exercise, weight, and rep data. A map view shows pins at each location. The message: one device, every gym, complete history.

7. **[0:52-0:65] End card.** Both products on a dark surface. "Any gym. Anywhere. IronPal." Kickstarter call to action.

**Music:** Upbeat, energetic, slightly adventurous. A sense of movement and freedom. Think: travel ad meets fitness montage.

**Voiceover:** "Your gym changes. Your training doesn't. IronPal goes wherever you go — no installation, no cameras on the wall, no app to the specific gym. One wearable. Every gym. Your complete workout history, everywhere."

**Mood:** Freedom, independence, versatility. Appeals to gym-hoppers, travelers, and anyone who trains at multiple locations.

### Video Prompt 5 — "60 Seconds of Nothing" (Minimalist Product Film)

**Duration:** 60 seconds

**Concept:** A pure aesthetic product film — no voiceover, no text (except the final logo), no explanations. Just the product and a single athlete in a beautifully shot, silent gym session. The camera work tells the story entirely visually. Designed to be hauntingly beautiful and scroll-stopping on social media.

**Shot list:**

1. **[0:00-0:08]** Extreme slow motion. An empty gym at dawn — golden light streaming through windows, hitting the chrome of barbells and machines. Dust particles visible in the light beams. Silence except a low ambient hum.

2. **[0:08-0:15]** An athlete's hand reaches into a gym bag and pulls out the IronPal headband. Slow motion. The fabric unfolds. The camera module catches the light — a glint on the glass lens. The "IronPal" text is visible as the headband rotates in the hand.

3. **[0:15-0:20]** The athlete puts the headband on. Close-up of the back of the head — hands adjusting the fit. Cut to front — the tiny lens centered on the forehead, the teal LED blinking once.

4. **[0:20-0:42]** A continuous, fluid workout sequence shot in cinematic slow motion with a steadicam orbit. The athlete moves through exercises — no cuts, one flowing take (or appearing as one):
   - Steps under the barbell at the squat rack, unracks, squats — camera orbits from side to behind
   - Walks to the cable machine, sets the pin (close-up insert of the pin sliding in), performs lat pulldown — camera orbits from front to side
   - Picks up dumbbells (label visible: "20 KG"), performs shoulder press — camera rises to a high angle looking down
   
   The headband with the teal LED glowing is visible throughout — a consistent teal accent in every frame.

5. **[0:42-0:48]** The athlete finishes. Slow motion: sweat on the forehead, a deep exhale, a slight smile. They pull the headband down around their neck. The teal LED fades off.

6. **[0:48-0:55]** The phone on the bench lights up. A notification: "Workout logged — 6 exercises, 18 sets." The athlete glances at it. Doesn't even pick it up. They already know.

7. **[0:55-0:60]** Fade to black. IronPal logo. Teal LED pulse.

**Music:** A single, haunting piano or ambient synth piece. Minimal. Emotional. Not "gym music" — something unexpected and arresting. Think: Nils Frahm or Ólafur Arnalds.

**Voiceover:** None. Pure visual storytelling.

**Mood:** Meditative, premium, art-film quality. Makes the viewer feel something rather than think something. Designed to stop the scroll and create desire.

### Video Prompt 6 — "The Numbers" (Data-Driven Pitch / Investor Angle)

**Duration:** 75-90 seconds

**Concept:** A fast-paced, information-dense video that leads with the problem (statistics about workout logging abandonment), shows IronPal as the solution, and closes with the business case. Designed for a Kickstarter audience that wants to understand not just what the product does, but why it will succeed. Uses kinetic typography, data visualizations, and product shots intercut.

**Shot list:**

1. **[0:00-0:08] Opening stat.** Black screen. Animated teal text appears with kinetic typography: "67% of gym-goers quit logging their workouts within 3 months." Beat. "The reason? It's too much work."

2. **[0:08-0:15] The problem visualized.** Quick montage of hands typing on phones between sets, confused faces trying to remember weights, abandoned workout apps with empty entries. Slightly desaturated color grading.

3. **[0:15-0:22] The solution — product reveal.** The IronPal headband and cap emerge from darkness (Apple-style). Teal LED glows. "IronPal" logos visible. Animated text: "A wearable camera that logs your workout automatically."

4. **[0:22-0:35] How it works — three pillars.** Three rapid segments, each 4-5 seconds:
   - **"SEES the exercise"** — POV shot of the camera viewing a chest press machine, teal bracket highlighting the machine → app showing "Chest Press" detected
   - **"READS the weight"** — POV shot of a weight stack with pin, teal bracket on the number "45" → app showing "45 kg"

5. **[0:35-0:45] Cost + tech stats.** Animated infographic-style visuals on dark background:

7. **[0:55-0:70] The ask.** Product shots. Both headband and cap. "IronPal" logo. Animated tier pricing:

8. **[0:70-0:80] End card.** "Back us on Kickstarter. Ship date: [quarter]." IronPal logo. Teal LED pulse.

**Music:** Energetic, confident electronic beat with a data/tech feel. Think: startup pitch video music. Fast-paced to match the kinetic typography.

**Voiceover:** "Two out of three gym-goers give up on tracking their workouts. Not because they don't care — because logging is a chore. IronPal changes that. A tiny camera in a headband or cap that sees your exercises, reads your weights, and counts your reps — automatically. Powered by AI. Less than a penny per workout. No gym installation. No manual input. Just put it on, and lift. Back us on Kickstarter."

**Mood:** Urgent, data-backed, convincing. Appeals to the logical/analytical backer who wants to see the numbers before pledging.

## Selected Video Prompt 1: "Your Workout, Logged Before You Leave" (Prompt #2)

**Weighted Score: 9.00 — Highest overall**

### Why This Is #1

This is the **primary Kickstarter campaign video**. It earns the top score because:

- **Perfect problem/solution arc.** Every gym-goer has experienced the frustration of manual workout logging — pausing sets to type, forgetting reps, abandoning the habit. The "before" half immediately creates identification and empathy. The "after" half delivers the payoff. This 1-2 punch is the most proven narrative structure in crowdfunding.
- **Highest value proposition clarity (10/10).** Within 20 seconds, any viewer understands: "This thing logs my workout so I don't have to." No ambiguity, no tech jargon needed.
- **Strongest conversion driver.** The before/after format directly triggers the "solve my problem" motivation that drives Kickstarter pledges. The viewer goes from "I hate logging" to "take my money" in 75 seconds.
- **Highly feasible.** Needs one gym, 2-3 athletes, a phone, and the prototype product. No complex multi-location shoots or elaborate post-production.
- **Works as the page hero video.** This is what plays when someone lands on the Kickstarter page. It must explain the product, create desire, and convert — in under 90 seconds. Prompt #2 does all three.

**Recommended use:** Main Kickstarter campaign page video (the one visitors see first).

## Selected Video Prompt 2: "What the Camera Sees" (Prompt #3)

**Weighted Score: 8.15 — Best educational/proof video**

### Why This Is #2

This video directly addresses the **biggest skepticism hurdle**: "Can a head-mounted camera really see enough to track a workout?" Instead of claiming it works, this video *shows proof*.

- **Highest shareability for a demo video.** The split-screen format (athlete + POV) is visually novel and immediately compelling. People will share this because it's satisfying to watch — like a magic trick where you see both sides.
- **Strongest credibility builder.** Kickstarter backers are burned by vaporware. Showing the actual camera POV with readable weight labels, identifiable exercises, and visible rep cycles → app output is the most honest, trust-building content possible.
- **Addresses the "how does it work?" question.** After watching Prompt #2 (the hero video), the first question is "but how?" This video answers it visually — no technical explanation needed.
- **Lower feasibility score (6/10) is manageable.** The split-screen POV requires actual footage from a body-mounted camera, which means a working prototype. For MVP, even phone-on-headband test footage would work. The POV doesn't need to be polished — slightly raw POV footage actually increases authenticity.

**Recommended use:** Second video on the Kickstarter page (below the fold, in the "How It Works" section). Also excellent for social media teasers — the split-screen format works perfectly as a 15-30 second clip.

## Selected Video Prompt 3: "The Numbers" (Prompt #6)

**Weighted Score: 7.95 — Best conversion closer for analytical backers**

### Why This Is #3

The first two videos sell the dream and show the proof. This video **closes the deal** for backers who need data before they pledge.

- **Highest Kickstarter conversion score (10/10).** This is the only video that includes pricing tiers, tech specs, cost economics, and accuracy stats. These are the details that turn interest into action — especially on Kickstarter, where backers evaluate campaigns like mini-investments.
- **Complements the other two perfectly.** Prompt #2 creates desire (emotional), Prompt #3 shows proof (visual), Prompt #6 delivers the business case (logical). Together, they cover all three decision-making modalities.
- **"< $0.01 per workout" is a killer line.** The AI cost-effectiveness message is uniquely compelling for a tech-savvy Kickstarter audience. It signals that this isn't just a cool demo — it's an economically viable product.
- **Highly feasible (8/10).** Kinetic typography and animated stats can be produced with After Effects or Motion. The product shots are reusable from Prompt #2. No additional gym footage needed — this is primarily a motion graphics piece with product shot inserts.
- **Works as a standalone social ads video.** The fast-paced, information-dense format performs well as a paid social ad on Facebook/Instagram, targeting fitness tech early adopters.

**Recommended use:** Third video on the Kickstarter page (in a "Why Back Us" or "Tech Specs" section). Also the primary video for paid social media advertising.

## Campaign Video Strategy Summary

| Position | Video | Purpose | Audience Trigger |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hero (top of page)** | #2 — "Your Workout, Logged" | Explain the product, create desire | Emotional: "That's my problem!" |
| **How It Works** | #3 — "What the Camera Sees" | Show proof, build trust | Visual: "Oh wow, it actually works" |
| **Why Back Us** | #6 — "The Numbers" | Close the deal, justify the pledge | Logical: "The numbers check out" |

### Why the Other Three Were Cut

- **#1 "The Reveal"** — Beautiful but too abstract for a Kickstarter page video. It creates desire but doesn't explain the product clearly enough for a first-time viewer. Better suited as a post-launch brand film or a teaser trailer 2 weeks before campaign launch.
- **#4 "Any Gym, Anywhere"** — Great concept but requires 4+ location shoots, which dramatically increases production cost and complexity. The multi-gym portability message can be communicated in a single slide or a few shots within Video #2 instead of needing its own dedicated video.
- **#5 "60 Seconds of Nothing"** — The most visually stunning option but scores lowest on value proposition clarity and conversion. It's an art piece, not a sales tool. Perfect for a brand Instagram reel post-launch, but too risky as Kickstarter campaign content where every second must drive toward a pledge.
